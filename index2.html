Various Perspectives Regarding the Establishment of Religious Truths


There are three classical approahces to addressing how one can come to confident beliefs in religious truths. These include Fideism, strong rationalism, and critical rationalism.


'Fideism' is the view that religious beliefs are not open to rational valuations and that they should be accepted withot question on the basis of faith. This is a gross misrepresentation of the Christianity. A number of issues are at play here: the mischaracterization of religion in general has commonplace in the sense that any religion calls for the individual to silence their thinking minds; faith is devoid of reason. This is not true. In fact, Christianity has within it a rich history of intellectual giants, many of whom had this conversation with both those inside the church and without. Additionally, a fault undersanding of the concept of faith has played a role shaping some of the popularly held conceptions of what faith is today. The Christian faith is cenetered on an object: a person, the person of God in Jesus Christ of Nazareth. Faith in Christianity does not act in a purely rational (or as some would say, a non-rational) sense. Faith is an act that is dependant on the character of God. Faith is placed upon, is rested upon, the person of God as one comes to see what he is like and what it is he has done in Jesus Christ. To exercise faith is to place the weight of your trust on God Himself. Such an act requires the mind, as such an act has a prerequisite of undersanding: the indiividual must understand God's message, what it means, and what it requires of an individual.


One of my professors used an example that clearly showed the issues with a fidesitic view In an imaginary conversation, a person explained how there was a little green man living inside the moon. When someone else questioned him regarding his strange belief, he explained that he had no justification for his belief, he simply believed it. He went on to say that his listener should simply believe it too. While this is an extreme example, the point is obvious. I have often thought to myself, should I find myself in a situation where I am at a table with a Mislim and a Buddhist, how would that conversation go? Ener any two other religious beliefs here and ask yourelf, who would be correct, how would their correctness be justified and how is it important that the correct religious view (should there be one) should be held by people? How is it an issue that wrong religious views are held and acted upon? Many people have a quick answer to this one.


On the other side of this spectrum is what is known as 'strong rationalism.' This view holds that faith can be shown as demonstrably true through reason and evidence. In real time, this equates to the position that 'any reasonable person should be convinced by my aguments.' Many in the faith held to this view. They thought that through their arguments Christianity could be shown to be absolutely true and that those who did not come to the faith through such means were being irrational. Christians and others have held to strong ratiaonlist views. Many scientists are strong rationalists. Bertrand Russel was a strong rationalist and went on to state that their are no valid arguments for religion; the only motivator for religion is the fear of death. Victor Stenger, who was a prticle physicist, philosophyer and author held that science disproved the existence of God. Beyond the fact that proving the nonexistence of something is quite the task, taking such an approach is not as straight forward as one may think. Often, those who hold to a strong rationalist view inevitably overstate their case. Some may unknowingly become close-minded as they lose their willingness to readjust according to new data, especially in the sense that anything outside of the realm of reason cannot speak into this matter. While reason is important, a human experience that rests solely on reason will be handicapped - throughout our lives there are many experiences that any in given moment cannot be addressed solely through the faculty or reason.


A thrid view is called 'critical rationalism' This view holds that religious claims are open to rational investigation. It is not necessary to absolutely prove a claim, but it is important to be able to give good arguments for a claim and defend said claim as being valid and intelligable. This approach results in one holding a view whose position is justifiable. As opposed to holding that a view can be irrifutably proven to the point that only someone who is intellectually deficient would not hold said belief, one can maintain a belief with good reason. Inductive probability and the explanation of evidence and data come in to play when seeking to justify a belief in such a manner. We move from absolute certainty to having strong reasons for holding a belief. In this view, one may reject Christianity while still being rational. A person can reject a given religious position and do so not as a result of their being irrational. It is possible that something beyond the rationality is at play, be it a moral issue or a cognitive pathology such as pride. In short, the critical rationalist approach does not seek to prove in the hardest sense the truth of Christianity, but they hold to the beleif that arguments can be properly grounded and that those arguments are sufficient in leading one to a confident belief in the validity of Christianity.


Some believers hold that apologetics is unbiblical and go further in holding that it removes the need of the Holy Spirit's work in drawing a person to God in Christ.


For the believer, it can be of great benefit to set aside time to think on why they believe what they believe. There are myriad ways that one may come to a place of decision and faith in Christ. A Framework for Approaching Inquiry into God's Existence


Objective Approaches

Above I addressed the idea of hard proof. In a similar vein, that issue must be kept in mind when approaching how one determines if God exists. It was often the case in history for people defending their position to call upon 'proofs' to settle the case. The concept of proof, in the purest sense, finds its origins in mathematics. The concept of proof addressed those things which could be shown with certainty; they could be proved by mathematical steps which led inevitably to a conclusion which would always be the end result; an outcome was proved to be certain, showing something was true. As an example, if you have one thing and you place it together with another one thing, you will cerainly always have two things. In logic, certain truths can be established in a similar (albeit still different)

manner. The laws of identity, excluded-middle and non-contradiction are examples of this. Beyond this, it becomes hard to absolutely prove something to be true. Without getting too deep into philosophy here and addressing what can be known, etc., I will only say that we are left with establihing conclusions which are reasonable. We can determine what is liklely, what is justifibly believed and we can act out of those convictions. For those who want to delve a bit deeper into this, you can access our page on the scientific method here.

An objective approach that can be taken when trying to determine the reasonableness of God's existence can be termed the best explanation approach. This lies behind much of science and is the stance held by many who hold to a theory of evolution: evolution serves as a framework which best explains the reality in which we exist. It is important to understand this when trying to have humble and fruitful conversations with those who hold to another view their you own. The best explanation approach stands on finding the best explanation among a number of explanations which does a better jobs making sense of the world we live in when compared to the other explanations. This process takes in all the evidences we have and attempts to explain all those evidences simultaneously in the most reasonable fashion. The best explanation will, compared to other explanations, do the best job of satisfying the critera of

    Explanatory Scope:

    Explanatory scope addresses the breadth of facts and issues which are addressed. The explanation which addresses the greater scope of details will be the better explanation overall. If an explanation cannot speak to the number of details that another explanation can, that explanation is less suited to speak to reality. The explanation which addresses the most issues is more likely to be true.


    Explanatory Power:

    Explanatory power speaks to the overall quality of the explanation. The explanation with the greatest power to speak to a given set of details will lack ambiguity and vaguness and will not require strained efforts that border on contrivance. The overall quality of the explanation speaks to an explanation's power.


    Plausability:

    Plausability takes into account those things which are alredy known. An explanation with greater plasuability will will fit better with the background knowledge we already have. An explanation that is more plausable, given the background knowledge that we already have is going to be the better explanation.


    Least Ad Hoc:

    An Ad Hoc explanation is one that is created 'on the fly' so to speak, it is an explanation that serves more to suit the situation than to get to the truth. Ad Hoc explanations typically have no general application and are used in the moment to defend an explanation, often with imagination and creativity, at the expense of prioritzing the explanation that is actually true. It is more concerned with maintaing a position for the sake of the position and is less concerned with the a positions relation to what is true. The explanation which is least Ad Hoc is going to be the explanation that has greater vailidity and grounding in truth.


    Illumination:

    Illumination is the ability of an explanation to speak into other areas. An explanation that shed's light into other areas is going to the better exlpanation based on the criteria of illumination. Often, these explanations will continually speak into other issues as time goes on. In such a case, an explanation will not only speak to the question at hand but will also address a host of related issues. Some of these issues may include creation, morality, the human experience, consiousness, rationality, etc.
